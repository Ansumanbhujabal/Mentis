## Clinical Use & Mechanism

Beyond these standard uses, the solution has been explored in diverse surgical and perioperative contexts to optimize patient outcomes.

For instance, recent studies have considered its role in conjunction with agents like tranexamic acid for managing blood loss in orthopedic surgeries, particularly in elderly patients undergoing procedures such as those for pertrochanteric fractures. Although intravenous administration of normal saline with tranexamic acid has been recommended, the optimal dosing and administration routes remain subjects of clinical debate [source](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42027320/).

## Risks & Alternatives

0.9% Sodium Chloride (Normal Saline) is a widely used intravenous fluid, valued for its isotonic properties with an osmolality of 308 mOsm/L and a specific composition of sodium (154 mEq/L) and chloride (154 mEq/L) [6](https://nurseslabs.com/iv-fluids/). However, its application is not without risks. Administering more than 1L per day may exceed physiological sodium and chloride levels, potentially causing **hypernatremia** and **hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis** [2](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK545210/). Furthermore, rapid correction of hypernatremia in adapted patients can lead to cerebral edema, posing a critical safety concern [1](https://emeamedicationdelivery.baxter.com/sites/g/files/ebysai3906/files/2022-10/ie-spc-nacl0.9-clean.pdf). There is also emerging evidence suggesting links between normal saline use and increased risks of kidney injury and metabolic acidosis due to its relatively high chloride concentration compared to "balanced" alternatives like Ringer’s Lactate or Plasma-Lyte A [4](https://www.technologynetworks.com/drug-discovery/news/safer-alternative-to-saline-iv-fluid-prevents-kidney-injury-in-hospital-patients-363186). 

To mitigate these risks, healthcare providers are considering alternatives. Balanced crystalloid solutions, which closely mimic physiological conditions, have shown promise in reducing metabolic disruptions while maintaining efficacy [5](https://coreem.net/journal-reviews/salt-ed/). While normal saline remains a cornerstone of intravenous therapy, its side effects must be carefully weighed against its intended clinical outcomes, especially in high-risk populations such as patients with congestive heart failure or severe renal insufficiency [3](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2017/016366s214lbl.pdf).
