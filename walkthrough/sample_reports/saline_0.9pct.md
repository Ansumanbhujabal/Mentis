## Risks & Alternatives

The use of 0.9% saline, also known as normal saline, carries several risks, particularly for vulnerable patient populations. Among the most significant concerns is [fluid overload](https://biomedicus.gr/sodium-chloride-0-9-side-effects-5/), which can lead to hypervolemia and exacerbate conditions like congestive heart failure. This issue arises due to the solution's isotonic nature, which traps water in extracellular spaces. Further risks include the potential for [electrolyte imbalances](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK545210/) such as hypernatremia and hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, particularly for patients receiving infusions exceeding 1 liter per day.

Studies also highlight that prolonged use of normal saline increases the likelihood of [metabolic acidosis and acute kidney injuries](https://www.technologynetworks.com/drug-discovery/news/safer-alternative-to-saline-iv-fluid-prevents-kidney-injury-in-hospital-patients-363186), presumably due to its higher sodium and chloride levels relative to blood. Consequently, careful patient monitoring is essential, especially for individuals with renal insufficiency or those predisposed to edema and sodium retention.

As alternatives to traditional normal saline, research suggests that balanced IV fluids with lower chloride concentrations might reduce risks such as kidney injury and acidosis. Nevertheless, the adoption of these safer alternatives remains limited, potentially due to [cost considerations](https://labeling.pfizer.com/ShowLabeling.aspx?id=4612), variations in clinical practice, or logistical constraints. Effective procurement strategies should weigh these risks against the widespread utility and affordability of normal saline in clinical and non-clinical applications.
