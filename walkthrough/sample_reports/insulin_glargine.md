## Clinical Use & Mechanism

Insulin glargine 300 U/mL, marketed as Toujeo, is a long-acting basal insulin designed for once-daily subcutaneous administration to manage blood glucose levels in individuals with type 1 and type 2 diabetes. Its concentrated formulation allows for smaller injection volumes, which can help improve patient comfort and adherence.

Toujeo has been compared with novel once-weekly basal insulins like efsitora alfa. While Toujeo requires daily administration, efsitora alfa aims to reduce injection frequency through its extended half-life and flat pharmacokinetic profile. Evidence from the QWINT phase 3 program indicates that once-weekly efsitora demonstrates similar efficacy and safety compared to daily basal insulins like glargine and degludec in type 2 diabetes management [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41591636/). Another systematic review underlines efsitora's potential to improve adherence by reducing injection frequency, though data on its long-term efficacy and safety remain limited [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41549720/).

## Risks & Alternatives

Insulin glargine, particularly in its high-strength formulation Toujeo (300 units/ml), plays a critical role in managing diabetes, especially in cases requiring long-acting insulin. However, its usage is not without risks. The primary concern is the potential for hypoglycemia, which may occur due to missed meals, excessive physical activity, or alcohol consumption. Hypoglycemia symptoms include diaphoresis, nausea, palpitations, and tremors, escalating in severity if left untreated. This risk is heightened during regimen changes, such as altering the insulin strength or switching from alternatives like Lantus, due to pharmacokinetic differences [source](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK557756/) [source](https://products.sanofi.us/insulin-glargine/insulin-glargine_winthrop.pdf).

A significant alternative to Toujeo is the first-in-class medication tirzepatide (Mounjaro), which activates GLP-1 and GIP receptors and is administered weekly. While not insulin, it offers improved glycemic control in type 2 diabetes. However, biosimilars to insulin glargine, such as Abasaglar and Semglee, are also viable options, though they are not bioequivalent to Toujeo and exhibit different durations of action [source](https://www.webmd.com/diabetes/diabetes-non-insulin) [source](https://www.birminghamandsurroundsformulary.nhs.uk/docs/ACG/Insulin%20risk%20assessment%20for%20high%20strength,%20biosimilar%20and%20fixed%20combinations%20V2.0%20May%202021.pdf).

For patient safety, education around the proper use and risks of high-strength insulin like Toujeo is paramount.
